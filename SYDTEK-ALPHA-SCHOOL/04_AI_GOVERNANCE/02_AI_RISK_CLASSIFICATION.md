# Purpose

The Artificial Intelligence Risk Classification Standard establishes how SydTek Alpha School classifies, evaluates, approves, monitors, and reviews AI systems according to institutional risk.

Not every AI system presents the same level of educational or operational risk.

Institutional governance should apply oversight proportional to the potential impact of each system.

This standard supports responsible innovation while protecting learners, faculty, staff, institutional operations, and public trust.

---

# Risk Philosophy

Risk classification is based on the **potential impact of AI-assisted decisions**, not merely on technical complexity.

Higher-impact systems require greater:

- human oversight;
- documentation;
- testing;
- approval;
- monitoring;
- auditing.

Lower-risk systems may follow simplified governance procedures.

---

# Guiding Principles

Risk classification should be:

- evidence based;
- transparent;
- repeatable;
- proportional;
- documented;
- periodically reviewed;
- institutionally governed.

Risk classifications may change as systems evolve.

---

# Risk Categories

SydTek adopts a four-tier institutional model.

```text
Level 1 — Minimal Risk

↓

Level 2 — Moderate Risk

↓

Level 3 — High Risk

↓

Level 4 — Critical Risk
```

Governance requirements increase with each level.

---

# Level 1 — Minimal Risk

Definition:

AI systems with limited educational or operational consequences.

Examples include:

- grammar assistance;
- meeting summaries;
- note organization;
- scheduling support;
- document formatting;
- instructional brainstorming.

Characteristics:

- no direct learner impact;
- easily reversible;
- minimal privacy implications.

Approval:

Departmental approval is generally sufficient.

---

# Level 2 — Moderate Risk

Definition:

AI systems that influence instructional experiences but do not independently determine academic outcomes.

Examples:

- AI tutoring;
- adaptive practice;
- study recommendations;
- curriculum navigation;
- educational search;
- research assistants.

Characteristics:

- educational influence;
- limited institutional consequences;
- human review readily available.

Approval:

Academic and technical review recommended before deployment.

---

# Level 3 — High Risk

Definition:

AI systems supporting consequential institutional decisions.

Examples include:

- admissions recommendations;
- academic advising support;
- competency evaluation assistance;
- financial aid recommendations;
- institutional analytics influencing resource allocation.

Characteristics:

- significant learner impact;
- institutional consequences;
- heightened privacy considerations.

Requirements:

- documented risk assessment;
- faculty oversight where applicable;
- executive approval;
- continuous monitoring.

Human review is mandatory before consequential decisions are implemented.

---

# Level 4 — Critical Risk

Definition:

AI systems associated with decisions carrying substantial legal, academic, financial, or institutional consequences.

Examples include:

- automated degree certification;
- employment termination recommendations;
- disciplinary adjudication;
- credential issuance;
- institutional policy enforcement.

Institutional policy prohibits fully autonomous operation of these functions.

Human decision-makers retain final authority.

---

# Risk Assessment Criteria

Each AI system should be evaluated across multiple dimensions.

Examples include:

- learner impact;
- faculty impact;
- institutional impact;
- privacy;
- security;
- accessibility;
- academic integrity;
- financial implications;
- legal implications;
- operational dependency.

Risk assessments should be documented before production deployment.

# Required Documentation

Every AI system should document:

- risk classification;
- rationale;
- intended use;
- known limitations;
- mitigation strategies;
- approval authority;
- review schedule.

Documentation supports accountability and future audits.

---

# Human Oversight Requirements

Oversight expectations increase with risk.

| Risk Level | Human Oversight |
|------------|----------------|
| Level 1 | Periodic review |
| Level 2 | Faculty or staff oversight |
| Level 3 | Mandatory human review before consequential action |
| Level 4 | Human decision required; AI advisory only |

Institutional authority may not be delegated to AI.

---

# Testing Requirements

Prior to deployment:

Level 1:

- functional testing.

Level 2:

- instructional testing;
- faculty validation.

Level 3:

- pilot implementation;
- bias review;
- privacy review;
- accessibility review.

Level 4:

- executive approval;
- governance committee review;
- legal review where appropriate;
- comprehensive monitoring plan.

Testing requirements should match institutional risk.

---

# Monitoring

Approved AI systems should be monitored for:

- accuracy;
- reliability;
- learner outcomes;
- faculty feedback;
- accessibility;
- privacy compliance;
- security;
- unexpected behavior.

Higher-risk systems require more frequent review.

---

# Reclassification

Risk classifications should be reconsidered when:

- functionality changes;
- educational purpose changes;
- new data sources are introduced;
- regulations change;
- institutional experience identifies new risks.

Reclassification follows institutional governance procedures.

---

# Incident Response

Significant AI incidents should trigger:

- immediate documentation;
- appropriate notification;
- temporary suspension where necessary;
- root-cause analysis;
- corrective actions;
- governance review.

Institutional learning should follow every significant incident.
