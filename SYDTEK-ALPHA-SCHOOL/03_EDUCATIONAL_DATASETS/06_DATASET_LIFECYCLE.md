---

# Purpose

The Educational Dataset Lifecycle Standard defines how educational datasets are conceived, developed, reviewed, approved, implemented, maintained, archived, and retired throughout their institutional lifespan.

A standardized lifecycle ensures instructional quality, academic consistency, AI reliability, version control, accreditation readiness, and continuous institutional improvement.

Every educational dataset follows the same documented lifecycle regardless of educational level.

---

# Guiding Principles

The dataset lifecycle should be:

- repeatable;
- transparent;
- faculty governed;
- evidence driven;
- learner centered;
- AI compatible;
- accreditation ready;
- continuously improving.

Datasets should evolve intentionally rather than through informal edits.

---

Each phase produces documented evidence.

---

# Phase 1 — Need Identification

Dataset development begins when an instructional need is identified.

Possible triggers include:

- new curriculum;
- revised competency;
- accreditation recommendations;
- faculty proposal;
- learner feedback;
- employer input;
- technological advances;
- regulatory changes.

Every proposal should include a documented educational rationale.

---

# Phase 2 — Planning

Planning establishes the dataset scope.

Planning activities include:

- defining learning outcomes;
- identifying competencies;
- determining prerequisites;
- selecting instructional methods;
- identifying assessment strategies;
- establishing accessibility requirements;
- defining AI support requirements.

Planning documentation becomes part of the permanent project record.

---

# Phase 3 — Development

Development creates instructional materials.

Typical deliverables include:

- instructional content;
- multimedia;
- learning activities;
- projects;
- laboratory exercises;
- assessments;
- grading rubrics;
- AI context;
- metadata.

Drafts remain under version control.

---

# Phase 4 — Faculty Review

Qualified faculty evaluate the dataset.

Faculty review considers:

- academic accuracy;
- instructional clarity;
- competency alignment;
- assessment validity;
- accessibility;
- AI compatibility;
- developmental appropriateness;
- disciplinary standards.

Review comments should be documented.

---

# Phase 5 — Institutional Approval

Approved reviewers authorize publication.

Approval documentation should identify:

- approving authority;
- approval date;
- version number;
- implementation date;
- review cycle.

Approval authority depends on the significance of instructional changes.

---

# Phase 6 — Publication

After approval the dataset becomes available for instructional use.

Publication activities include:

- repository release;
- metadata validation;
- AI synchronization;
- faculty notification;
- documentation updates;
- archival of superseded versions.

Only approved datasets should be assigned to learners.

---

# Phase 7 — Instruction and Assessment

Published datasets support:

- classroom instruction;
- independent learning;
- AI tutoring;
- competency verification;
- formative assessment;
- summative assessment;
- learner analytics.

Instruction generates evidence supporting future improvements.

---

# Phase 8 — Continuous Improvement

Institutional evidence informs future revisions.

Evidence sources include:

- learner outcomes;
- assessment performance;
- faculty recommendations;
- learner surveys;
- employer feedback;
- AI tutoring analytics;
- accreditation reviews.

Continuous improvement should occur through documented revisions rather than informal changes.

---

# Phase 9 — Archival or Retirement

Datasets may eventually be:

- superseded;
- archived;
- consolidated;
- retired.

Retirement should document:

- retirement date;
- rationale;
- replacement dataset where applicable;
- archival location.

Historical instructional records remain available for authorized review.

---

# Lifecycle Documentation

Every dataset maintains lifecycle documentation.

Minimum records include:

- proposal;
- planning documents;
- review comments;
- approval records;
- publication history;
- change log;
- revision history;
- retirement record where applicable.

These records support transparency and accreditation.

---

# AI Synchronization

Whenever a dataset changes, associated AI resources should also be reviewed.

Synchronization includes:

- AI Context Dataset updates;
- prompt validation;
- terminology review;
- competency mapping verification;
- hallucination testing.

AI systems should not rely on outdated instructional guidance.

---

# Assessment Synchronization

Lifecycle management also verifies that:

- assessments remain aligned;
- rubrics remain accurate;
- mastery standards remain current;
- competency mappings remain valid.

Instruction and assessment should evolve together.

---

# Faculty Responsibilities

Faculty responsibilities include:

- proposing revisions;
- reviewing datasets;
- approving instructional quality;
- validating competencies;
- recommending retirement when appropriate.

Faculty governance remains central throughout the lifecycle.

---

# Quality Gates

Datasets should satisfy institutional quality gates before publication.

Recommended gates include:

- metadata complete;
- competencies mapped;
- assessments validated;
- accessibility reviewed;
- AI compatibility verified;
- licensing documented;
- approvals recorded.

Datasets failing quality gates should return to development.

---

# Metrics

Lifecycle effectiveness may be evaluated using:

- publication time;
- revision frequency;
- learner success;
- faculty satisfaction;
- AI performance;
- accessibility compliance;
- accreditation findings.

Metrics support continuous institutional improvement.
