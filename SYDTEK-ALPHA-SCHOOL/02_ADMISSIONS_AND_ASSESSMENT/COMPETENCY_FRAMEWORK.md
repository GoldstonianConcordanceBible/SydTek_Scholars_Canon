# Purpose

The Competency Framework establishes the official competency-based educational model used throughout SydTek Alpha School.

Rather than measuring educational success primarily through instructional time, the SydTek model measures learning through verified competency while maintaining appropriate legal, developmental, accreditation, and institutional requirements.

Competencies are the common language connecting:

- educational datasets;
- curriculum;
- assessment;
- AI tutoring;
- faculty review;
- pricing;
- credentials;
- institutional effectiveness.

---

# Guiding Principles

Every competency must be:

- measurable;
- observable;
- evidence based;
- developmentally appropriate;
- academically rigorous;
- consistently assessed;
- faculty verified where required;
- aligned with institutional learning outcomes.

Competency-based education supplements—not replaces—sound instructional practice.

---

# Definition of Competency

A competency is the demonstrated ability to apply:

- knowledge;
- skills;
- judgment;
- communication;
- ethical reasoning;
- problem solving;
- professional behavior;

to an approved standard.

Competency requires evidence.

Completion of instructional activities alone does not establish competency.

# Competency Levels

SydTek recognizes multiple levels of competency development.

## Level 1 — Awareness

The learner recognizes basic concepts.

Typical evidence:

- recall;
- identification;
- recognition;
- simple explanation.

---

## Level 2 — Understanding

The learner explains concepts correctly.

Evidence may include:

- written responses;
- discussions;
- demonstrations;
- guided activities.

---

## Level 3 — Application

The learner applies knowledge in authentic situations.

Examples:

- projects;
- laboratory work;
- case studies;
- simulations.

---

## Level 4 — Analysis

The learner evaluates, compares, investigates, and interprets information independently.

---

## Level 5 — Synthesis

The learner integrates multiple competencies to solve complex problems.

---

## Level 6 — Innovation

The learner creates original work demonstrating independent mastery and scholarly or professional contribution.

Innovation is generally expected within advanced graduate and doctoral education.

# Grade-Level Competencies

## Kindergarten–Grade 2

Focus on:

- foundational literacy;
- numeracy;
- communication;
- curiosity;
- social development;
- responsible technology awareness.

---

## Grades 3–5

Expand competencies in:

- reading comprehension;
- mathematical reasoning;
- scientific observation;
- writing;
- collaboration.

---

## Grades 6–8

Introduce:

- research foundations;
- project-based learning;
- digital citizenship;
- analytical thinking;
- career awareness.

---

## Grades 9–12

Students develop:

- college readiness;
- workforce readiness;
- financial literacy;
- AI literacy;
- advanced communication;
- interdisciplinary projects;
- capstone preparation.

---

# Competency Evidence

Competencies may be demonstrated through:

- examinations;
- projects;
- portfolios;
- laboratories;
- simulations;
- oral examinations;
- performances;
- case analyses;
- internships;
- clinical experiences where applicable;
- capstones;
- theses;
- dissertations.

Multiple forms of evidence improve assessment validity.

---

# Mastery Standards

Each competency must define:

- performance expectations;
- acceptable evidence;
- scoring criteria;
- minimum mastery threshold;
- reassessment procedures;
- faculty approval requirements.

Mastery thresholds remain consistent for all learners seeking the same credential.

---

# AI Support

Artificial Intelligence may assist learners by:

- explaining concepts;
- generating practice activities;
- recommending educational datasets;
- identifying learning gaps;
- supporting study planning.

AI does not independently certify competency.

---

# Faculty Verification

Faculty verify competencies involving:

- academic credit;
- graduation;
- licensure preparation;
- thesis approval;
- dissertation approval;
- consequential academic decisions.

Faculty judgment remains authoritative.

---

# Prior Learning

Competencies previously mastered may be recognized through:

- prior-learning assessment;
- challenge examinations;
- portfolio review;
- military training;
- industry certifications;
- professional experience.

Recognition requires documented evidence.

---

# Competency Mapping

Every competency is mapped to:

- educational datasets;
- learning outcomes;
- assessments;
- curriculum;
- credentials;
- institutional learning outcomes.

Mapping supports accreditation, assessment, and continuous improvement.

---

# Continuous Improvement

The competency framework is reviewed using evidence such as:

- student performance;
- faculty feedback;
- employer feedback;
- graduate outcomes;
- assessment reliability;
- AI performance;
- accreditation recommendations.

Competencies evolve while preserving academic rigor.
